Research

Research

Scholarship at the intersection of media credibility, visual culture, and communication.

Dr. Chopra’s research asks a single, urgent question: how does media shape what people believe? Her work runs along three connected lines. The first is the spread and credibility of fake viral videos and misinformation, the focus of her doctoral study. The second is visual and ethnographic method, including photography and videography as tools for understanding culture. The third is consumer behaviour and digital branding, drawn from her marketing scholarship.

What ties them together is a commitment to evidence. Her studies are built on original field data, large-scale surveys, and close case analysis, using both quantitative and qualitative methods. The aim is research that is rigorous but also useful, connecting communication theory with the way media actually works in people’s lives.

Doctoral research

PhD Thesis

A Study to Determine the Social Impact and Credibility of Fake Viral Videos on Social Media

Amity University Haryana · Amity School of Communication · 2024 · Supervisors: Manish Verma; Sumit Narula

The study highlights the rising issue of fake viral videos that are manipulated and spread in many forms: misinformation, disinformation, and deepfakes. People are connected through digital media, using platforms like Facebook, WhatsApp, and Twitter to share opinions, values, and beliefs across social, personal, and professional groups. Sharing on these platforms is becoming dangerous. With the help of audio-visual fake videos from unknown sources, content is shared widely to provoke people and create hoaxes with political, religious, entertainment, and informational framing.

Read the full abstract

The rise of social-media platforms has helped information spread quickly and at a huge scale. Along with the benefits of connection, these platforms have also seen a sharp rise in fake viral videos. This study explores how common they are and how they work: the ways they gain traction, the factors behind their virality, and the possible motivations behind their creation.

The study investigates the specific implications of fake viral videos on WhatsApp, Twitter, and Facebook, considering each platform's unique features and user dynamics. WhatsApp's private nature can foster the spread of misinformation within closed circles; Twitter's fast-paced, public nature can amplify false content; and Facebook, with its extensive user base and algorithmic curation, can inadvertently facilitate viral spread.

It also looks at the steps platforms take to reduce this impact, and the challenge of balancing free expression against curbing fake viral videos, and how this affects what social-media users believe. Fake viral videos pose serious challenges to public trust by distorting the flow of information and shaping social behaviour. They also reveal gaps in content moderation, algorithmic transparency, media-literacy efforts, and regulation.

Keywords: Fake, viral videos, propaganda, political, religious, entertainment, informational, riots, violence, society, social media users.
