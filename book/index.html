The Book

Manipulative Realities

Viral Propaganda of Digital Deception

When seeing is no longer believing, who decides what is real?

Manipulative Realities: Viral Propaganda of Digital Deception is a research-led book on how fake viral videos and deepfakes have broken the link between seeing and believing. It grows out of years of original doctoral research into the credibility of viral video and the behaviour of social-media audiences.

The title

Manipulative Realities names the problem at the heart of the book: a world where what we see can no longer be trusted. The subtitle, Viral Propaganda of Digital Deception, shows how it spreads. Not through a single liar, but through the network itself, at the speed of a share.

What the book is about

The book argues that digital deception is no longer a string of isolated lies. It has become structural, built into the way social media works. On platforms with no gatekeeper, the same engine of manipulation quietly reshapes three pillars of public life:

  • Entertainment, where morphed celebrity clips teach a billion people that video can lie, and that this is normal.
  • Religion, where the same tools turn deadly, and a single fake clip can spark real violence within hours.
  • Politics, where deception becomes organised, funded, and amplified by powerful, verified accounts.

Seen together, these are not three separate problems. They are three stages of one escalating pipeline, each preparing the ground for the next.

Why it matters now

We have entered the age of the deepfake, where misinformation, disinformation, and synthetic media spread faster than the truth can follow. The danger is no longer only that fake videos are believed. It is that real ones can now be dismissed as fake. Manipulative Realities explains how we reached this point, and why media literacy has become a basic civic skill.

The research behind it

This is a book built on evidence, not opinion. Its foundation is rare for a work written for a general audience:

  • A survey of 470 respondents across three generations, covering how each group consumes, trusts, and shares viral video.
  • Fifteen documented case studies of fake viral videos, each traced from origin to real-world impact.
  • In-depth interviews with five senior working journalists on credibility, gatekeeping, and verification.

Its central finding is stark: across every generation, almost no one checks whether a video is true before sharing it. The book reads that finding through a clear analytical lens, treating propaganda not as the act of a single bad actor, but as a property of the network itself.

Who it’s for

Educated general readers, journalism and media students, working media professionals, and policy researchers. It is written so that a Gen Z reader and a seasoned professor can both read it with equal reward.

A hopeful argument

The book does not end in despair. This is a society problem, not just a technology one, and society can answer it. The same generation that spreads manipulation fastest can be taught to stop it fastest. The answer is not to flee the digital world, but to live in it more carefully, critically, and bravely.
