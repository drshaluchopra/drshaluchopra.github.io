    - "The study highlights the rising issue of fake viral videos that are manipulated and spread in many forms: misinformation, disinformation, and deepfakes. People are connected through digital media, using platforms like Facebook, WhatsApp, and Twitter to share opinions, values, and beliefs across social, personal, and professional groups. Sharing on these platforms is becoming dangerous. With the help of audio-visual fake videos from unknown sources, content is shared widely to provoke people and create hoaxes with political, religious, entertainment, and informational framing."

Along with the benefits of connection, these platforms have also seen a sharp rise in fake viral videos. Dr. Chopra's research asks a single, urgent question: how does media shape what people
believe? Her work runs along three connected lines. The first is the spread and credibility of
fake viral videos and misinformation, the focus of her doctoral study. The second is visual and
ethnographic method, including photography and videography as tools for understanding culture.
The third is consumer behaviour and digital branding, drawn from her marketing scholarship.

What ties them together is a commitment to evidence. Her studies are built on original field
data, large-scale surveys, and close case analysis, using both quantitative and qualitative
methods. The aim is research that is rigorous but also useful, connecting communication theory
with the way media actually works in people's lives.
